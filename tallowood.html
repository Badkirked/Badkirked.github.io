  • Tallowwood is a moderate to large tree found in wet sclerophyll forests along coastal regions from the Hunter River district in New South Wales to Maryborough and Fraser Island in Queensland.
  • The heartwood ranges in color from pale to dark yellow-brown, sometimes with tinges of olive green, while the sapwood is whitish.
  • Moderately coarse texture with generally interlocked grain.
  • Free of gum veins and lacks prominent figure but has a distinctive luster and a 'greasy' appearance.
  • Exceptionally durable timber with a life expectancy of over 25 years in-ground and over 40 years above ground.
  • Highly resistant to decay and performs well in damp and wet conditions.
  • Sapwood is susceptible to lyctid borer (powder post beetle) attack.
  • Untreated Tallowwood is equivalent to fire-retardant-treated timber according to AS/NZS 3837.
  • Historically used for bearings, mallet heads, mauls, wheel spokes, and tool handles.
  • Current applications include wharf and bridge construction, railway sleepers, cross-arms, poles, piles, and mining timbers.
  • Used in construction for unseasoned framing, dressed timber cladding, internal and external flooring, linings, and joinery.
  • Utilized in fencing, landscaping, and retaining wall construction.
  • Decorative uses include outdoor furniture, turnery, and joinery.
  • Other applications encompass boat building, coach and carriage construction, agricultural machinery, and structural plywood.
  • Commonly found in:
    • New South Wales: Hunter River district, North Coast (Coffs Harbour, Port Macquarie, Byron Bay), Sydney
    • Queensland: Maryborough, Fraser Island, Brisbane, Gold Coast, Sunshine Coast, Far North Queensland (Cairns, Townsville)
